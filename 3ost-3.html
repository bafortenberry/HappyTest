Hacking College Credits

Here's a couple of student bios:

Triple major, 4.0, on track to graduate two semesters early and double major, foreign language minor, 3.9, graduated one semester early. How's it possible?

The answer is scooping up as much credit as possible. In addition to the other hacks and habits outlined in the better student blog (systems, environment, time management, etc.), these high performers know how to hack the game of college credits. Here are a few places to start.

CLEP (College Level Examination Program)

CLEP tests are a great way to get out of bs prereqs and core curriculum courses. First, figure out which required courses for your major have CLEP offerings. It’s usually any sort of intro level 100 class like intro to micro/macroeconomics, freshman biology, etc. Check your university registrar to determine which test will satisfy which credit and the minimum score necessary. Schedule the test, pay the fees (probably about 90$), and get a couple prep books to study up. We know one freshman who tested out of the entire non-major bio sequence (8 hours) and intro to macro (3 hours) over winter break.

Back Credit Via Placement Tests

We've seen students get 10 hours of back credit for testing into 300-level courses their freshman year. Even if you have to “stretch the rules” on the online placement test it’s well worth it. After you get everything scheduled talk to the relevant department about filling out paperwork to cash in on back credit. One student who already spoke Spanish maxed out the test and got 10 extra back credit ours her first semester.

Double Count

Example, you’re an honors college student majoring in finance. Finance major requires a threehour literature credit (*rolls eyes*) and the honors college requires six hours of honors college lectures. Take the honors college survey of literature and count it towards your finance major and your honors college requirements. This is a hypothetical, but the idea holds true. We’ve even seen triple counts where a student got required course, major course, and honors course credit for a single seminar.

Overload Schedule With Online Courses

This is a bit of a gamble, because it’s impossible to know beforehand exactly how much work a class will be. However, we generally operate under the assumption that online classes for undergraduates are total b*llsh*t. Two hours of online credit tends to be about as much work as one hour or less of traditional class. We can knock out a ton of easy classes in a single semester when we overload our schedule with them. Most departments cap semester hours at 18, but with a good GPA most of them will allow up to 21. Ask an adviser who to contact (usually the Dean’s office). Send them an email and any required info and get to work. We know a guy who knocked out all his required 300-level business intros and made the president’s list in an online-heavy 21-hour semester.

Establish a good relationship with your academic adviser.

He or she will be a helpful guide over the next few years. More importantly, he or she can help you bend the rules to get ahead of the game. One student got a required intro CS class written off because his adviser knew he understood the material and didn’t want to slow him down.
