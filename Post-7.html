Top 8 Books on Happiness

The works below were selected via an analysis of the most popular and well-regarded books on happiness.

The selection process referenced top sites associated with the Google term “happiness books”. From this point the top ten most relevant sites were selected which included reviews, recommendations, and rankings for happiness-related books. Featured works were then compiled into a spreadsheet which also contains the average rank and the amount of times each book was featured on a site. The results are shown below.

"Authentic Happiness" by Martin Seligman

We can improve happiness by altering feelings about the past: letting go of dogma, cultivating gratitude, and forgiving others. We can improve happiness by altering feelings about the future: training optimism and disputing negative thoughts. Finally, we can improve happiness by altering feelings about the present: focusing on gratification instead of pleasure. We achieve gratification and meaning by harnessing our signature strengths in service of something larger than ourselves. The pleasant life (positive emotions), good life (gratification from using strengths), and meaningful life (purpose in serving something larger) come together to form the full-life - the happy life.

...improve Happiness by altering feelings about present...

"10% Happier" by Dan Harris

Stripping away all the eccentricities of the west and mysterious elements of the east, meditation is just the practice of cultivating awareness of the present and of our internal conditions. It may not be a divine be-all-end-all; however, consistent practice can be a gateway to a greater capacity for mindfulness which leads to improved self-control, stability, contentment, and grace. Additionally, practicing non-attachment (enlightened self-interest), recognizing the truth of suffering, and avoiding multitasking will improve our well-being.

"The Art of Happiness: A Handbook for Living" by Cutler, Howard C. and Dalai Lama

There is a wealth of common-ground to be found in dialogues between east and west as shown in the discussion between the Dalai Lama and western psychologist, Howard Cutler. Drawing on the Buddhist idea that all living beings seek the cessation of suffering, we see that happiness is the purpose of life. This happiness is much more dependent on internal than external conditions. Further, it can be cultivated using a simple, systematic approach: pay attention to things that make you happy and unhappy and promote the former while eliminating the latter. Tapping into and cultivating the fundamental compassion underlying human nature will lead to a wellspring of sustainable joy.

"The How of Happiness: a Practical Guide to Getting the Life You Want" by Sonja Lyubomirsky

Happiness is 50% genetic (set-point), 10% external, and 40% intentional action, so it makes sense to focus on the variable that can be controlled to drive results – intention. The key to growing happy is not changing your genes or your circumstances but what you do and how you think. There exists a variety of happiness myths which tend to hold people back. Moving on to the importance of happiness, we see a multitude of benefits which come with happiness: increased sociability, charitability, cooperativeness, flexibility, success in love-life, health, and leadership ability.

"Stumbling on Happiness" by Daniel Gilbert

We cannot accurately predict what will lead us to be happy because the imagination struggles with detail, exhibits a too-similar-to-the-present bias, and fails to account for our ability to rationalize and cope. This last ability leads us humans to have a psychological immune system which maintains happiness levels. The last point is crucial to understanding happiness - we tend to subconsciously believe what makes us feel better and subconsciously ignore what makes us feel worse thus creating a sort of happiness homeostasis mechanism.

"The Happiness Project" by Gretchen Rubin

We must ask “what do we want from life anyway?” and begin to take action towards happiness. Insights can be found in philosophy, science, and above all real-world experience. By implementing goals, resolutions, and an accountability framework, such as a monthly practice goal, we can improve each element of our lives and thus change our lives for the better without any drastic life changes.

"The Happiness Advantage" by Shawn Achor

We tend to think of the relationship between happiness and success as success then happiness. Modern research actually finds the opposite - success is contingent upon happiness. Happy individuals exhibit superior creativity, motivation, productivity, etc. We should not prioritize success to find happiness, rather we should prioritize happiness to find success. By implementing positive psychology principles – conditioning, mindset, persistence, regaining control, habit modification, and social investment – individuals can improve their own happiness which leads greater success.

"The Happiness Hypothesis" by Jonathan Haidt

The seemingly infinite wisdom available today regarding happiness and the human condition can be consolidated into a top ten list from history’s greatest minds. This list touches on the divided-self which refers to the internal tension between our instinct and reason, our ability to transform the mind through therapy and meditation, the intricacy of our relationships (Golden Rule), our bias towards seeing faults in others and overlooking our own, the fact that happiness is depend on the internal and external, the importance of both love and adversity, and the power of virtue. Finally, we conclude that happiness emerges as the balanced harmony between all of these forces at play in the human experience.
